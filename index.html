Research Methodology

Step-by-step guide to collecting bibliometric data

1. Google Scholar — General counts:
Go to Google Scholar Advanced Search. In the "with the exact phrase" field, type each term. Set the date range to a single year (e.g., 2011–2011). Note the "About X results" number at the top. Repeat for each year in your range.

2. Journal-specific counts:
Use the same approach but add source:"Journal Name" to your query. Example: "your term" source:"Your Journal Name". The 🔗 links in this dashboard are pre-built for you.

3. Alternative tools for more rigorous results:
— Scopus or Web of Science: Allow precise filtering by journal, year, and keyword in title/abstract/keywords. They also export as CSV.
— ERIC (eric.ed.gov): Especially relevant for education journals.
— Publish or Perish (free software by Harzing): Queries Google Scholar programmatically and exports structured data with citation metrics.
— Dimensions (dimensions.ai): Free tier available, good for trend analysis.

4. Important caveats:
— Google Scholar counts are approximate (rounded) and can fluctuate day to day.
— The source: filter in Google Scholar is not perfectly reliable.
— For publication-quality data, Scopus or Web of Science are strongly preferred.
— Always record the date you collected data, as counts change over time.
— Partial-year data (e.g., the current year) will appear lower and should be noted.

5. Interpreting your charts:
— Line charts are best for spotting trends and turning points.
— Bar charts make year-to-year comparison across terms easier.
— Stacked area charts show how individual journals contribute to the total.
— Use the CSV/JSON export for further statistical analysis in R, Python, or Excel.

About This Tool

This is a free, open-source bibliometric research tool. It runs entirely in your browser — no data is sent to any server. Your data is saved in your browser's localStorage so it persists between sessions.
